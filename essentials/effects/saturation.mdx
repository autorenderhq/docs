## Overview

The saturation effect controls the intensity and vibrancy of colors in your images. Increase saturation for vibrant, colorful images, or decrease it for a more muted, desaturated look.

### URL Structure

AutoRender URLs with saturation follow this structure:

```bash
https://assets.autorender.io/LOKVTtKVGb/e_saturation:30/docs/example/porsche.jpg
```

## Saturation (`e_saturation`)

Adjusts the intensity/vibrancy of colors.

**Format:** `e_saturation:{level}`
- **Range:** `-100` to `100`
- **Negative values:** Desaturate (move toward grayscale)
- **Positive values:** Increase color intensity

## Use Cases

- **Vibrant images:** Increase saturation for colorful, eye-catching photos
- **Muted aesthetics:** Decrease saturation for a subtle, elegant look
- **Product photography:** Enhance colors to make products more appealing
- **Artistic effects:** Create vintage or desaturated looks

## Combining with Other Effects

Saturation works well with contrast and brightness:

```bash
https://assets.autorender.io/LOKVTtKVGb/e_saturation:25,e_contrast:20,e_brightness:10/docs/example/porsche.jpg
```

<img src="https://assets.autorender.io/LOKVTtKVGb/e_saturation:25,e_contrast:20,e_brightness:10/docs/example/porsche.jpg" alt="Saturation with contrast and brightness" class="ar-url-image" />

## Best Practices

- Moderate saturation (20-40) usually looks most natural
- High saturation can make images look oversaturated
- Negative values create a desaturated, vintage look
- Combine with other color adjustments for best results
